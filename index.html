work.

Moodify

For our third project, we were split into teams and asked to build a fully RESTful, MEAN stack app over the course of a week. After deciding to do something out of the ordinary that combined something we all loved (not an easy feat) we landed on Moodify. Moodify gives users a playlist from spotify depending on their mood. They are able to create an account/login (either via spotify or through the app directly) and upload a photo of themself via Filestack. It then sends this to a third party facial recognition API (Face++) which returns data on which emotion is detected at the highest level. Moodify then returns a playlist to match that mood such as “Sad Songs”, or “Get Home Happy!”. This was my favourite project over the course. I have a real affinity for working with third party API’s. There is something I love about taking data that is available for me and moulding it to do something entirely different and out of the ordinary. This was also our first group project which I loved because it’s clear how much more you can achieve in a team not just due to man hours but also coming down to the support that we gave each other from start to end.

Destination Love

Our final project brief was much like our previous, however we used React for this one rather than Angular, and we had the choice to work in groups or on our own. Myself and another friend from class decided to work together for a few reasons, namely that we both enjoyed working with other people and we knew we could achieve more together than we could on our own. I’ll say I was a lot more confident after spending more time on it with this project and I knew that would be a huge part of my life going forward as a developer. Destination Love is a fully RESTful, MERN stack “dating app” which allowed users to match with others, message those matched love interests, and, when the time was right, plan a date with them. When users decided to plan a date, Destination Love would use Google Maps to find the midpoint between the two lovers geographical locations, and return a list of bars within a 1km radius of that midpoint - essentially removing that “Where should we go?” dilemma. This was a really fun project to work on so I’m so glad I did it in a group, especially it being the final brief we were given. It was also the first project I took on Google Maps - a challenge but a very enjoyable one with that.

Sloth Burger

Our first project of the course. After tackling HTML, CSS and JavaScript for two and a half weeks, we were given the brief for our first project which was to build a game using what we had learnt so far. With such a wide scope of options I decided to combine two things I love most in life - sloths and burgers. The finished product was a collision based game which required the player to move the sloth at the bottom of the screen to collect burgers and score as many points as they could while at the same time avoiding the carrots as they deducted points. I was happy with the outcome but having hit MVP earlier than I expected I became fixated on having a leaderboard that wouldn’t refresh when the page was reloaded. As we hadn’t yet covered server side or databases I decided to utilise local storage to store the usernames and scores of the players. I really loved this project - not only because it was a combination of my two favourite things in life but it was fun and I learned so much. There were a lot of challenges I faced but it really solidified everything I had learned in the course so far.

InstaSpam

This was our first look into Full Stack Develoment and what really piqued my interest in server side technologies. The finished product was a fully RESTful web application in which users could securely create an account and login, then upload, view, edit, delete and comment on pictures that had been uploaded. The name came from the idea that all the content would be inspired by “Spam” the canned meat, and was a parody version of the instagram account. Interacting with the server side in this project was something I really enjoyed as well as the authentication side of things.
